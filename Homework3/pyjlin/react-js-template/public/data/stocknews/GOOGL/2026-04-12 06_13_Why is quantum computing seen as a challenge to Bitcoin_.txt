Cocoa prices are falling — but not in time for Easter (CNN Business) Investing.com --The rise of cryptographically relevant quantum computers (CRQCs) is no longer a distant "decade away" concern, but for Bitcoin and the broader digital asset ecosystem, the threat appears manageable. According to a new deep-dive report from Bernstein, recent breakthroughs in quantum computing have accelerated timelines, but the transition to post-quantum security is expected to be an "orderly upgrade cycle" rather than a catastrophic event. The report notes that recent developments, specifically a paper from Google Quantum AI demonstrating a 20-fold reduction in the qubits required to break modern encryption, have shifted expectations. However, Bernstein analysts Gautam Chhugani and Mahika Sapra argue that the jump from dozens of logical qubits to the thousands required to threaten Bitcoin’s ECDSA (Elliptic Curve Digital Signature Algorithm) is a multi-dimensional engineering challenge that will take years to solve. "The scaling from 10s of logical qubits to 1000s of logical qubits is not trivial and involves multi-dimensional breakthroughs," the Bernstein report states. "Quantum timelines may still be more optimistic than reality." Analysts suggest that the massive commercial costs, potentially reaching hundreds of billions of dollars, and the complexity of physical hardware mean the industry has a viable 3-to-5-year window to transition to quantum-resistant protocols. Bitcoin’s solution lies in a "soft fork" upgrade that would introduce post-quantum cryptographic (PQC) signature schemes. The report indicates that the Bitcoin community is already exploring algorithms like SPHINCS+ or Lamport signatures, which are theoretically resistant to quantum Shor’s algorithm attacks. "Bitcoin and crypto protocols should prepare for post-quantum security, and we expect adequate time for protocols to evolve," Bernstein noted. The transition would likely involve users moving their funds from current "vulnerable" addresses to new, quantum-secure address types. The transition requires active user participation, and analysts believe the incentive to protect capital will ensure high adoption rates. Investors’ takeaway is that while the "Quantum Threat" remains a popular headline, the underlying technology has clear, albeit technical, paths toward long-term survival.
